My Journey to Web Design and Development

Writing was my first real passion. I always loved my English classes, and have been writing my own stories since elementary school. By the time I started high school, I was trying to figure out how I could build a career around my passion. I joined my high school newspaper and yearbook staffs where I was introduced to reporting, news writing, and print design. The faculty advisor for these staffs took us to camps, conferences, and competitions so we could learn new techniques from our student peers and industry professionals. Throughout this time, my interest started shifting from reporting to news design. I actually placed for news page design at regional, state, and national level competitions. By the fall of my senior year, I had decided to go to the University of Missouri and pursue a degree in journalism.

Before freshmen year was over, I knew I would not be pursuing reporting as a career. Instead, I would take the Multiplatform Design, which focus on news design for newspapers, magazines, and on websites. Several things about my degree planned changed over the years (I was going to double major in French, then double major in business with a minor in French, etc.), but my desire to design a platform that drew people's attention to the news content remained. In the fall of 2013, I took my first web design course. I loved it. Prior to this course, I had only considered working at newspapers or magazines. After the course, I knew I needed to include web design in my future.

I did end up double majoring. My second degree is a Bachelor's of Science and Business Administration with an emphasis in marketing. Honestly, this degree was added as back-up to ease my parents' worry that the "journalism thing" was not going to work out. However, the marketing courses I took complemented my courses in the journalism school. Where one taught me how to create content, the other covered how to get people to see it. In addition, I took courses about accounting, finance, and management which left with a working knowledge of the "nitty-gritty" side of business — something a lot of creatives and (young) journalists don't really have. My interest in marketing actually grew to the point where I had planned to pursue an MBA in market research.

The MBA plan was scrapped. By the time it came to start preparing for graduate school applications, my interest in the MBA had waned and I was burned out from 4.5 years (summers included) of undergrad. Instead, I completed my bachelor programs, graduated in December 2014, and promptly moved to Madison, Wisconsin. In Madison, I worked at the Lee Enterprises's Madison Regional Design Center as a news page design. I held this position for two years while trying to build my web design skills on my own time through online services such as Codecademy and Code School. While I loved a lot about my job, I reached the point where I was just showing up to do it. There weren't a lot of opportunities to grow and no opportunities to work on the news websites. I decided it was time to go back to school.

I started my course work at Anne Arundel Community College in January 2017. Since last year, I have taken classes introducing me to HTML and CSS, responsive web design, PHP and mySQL, Java, JavaScript, and jQuery. All of these courses have taken a hands-on approach so I have created 10+ multi-page websites for my web design classes and small applications for my programming ones. I will graduate in May 2018 with a Computer Science Associate's degree in Internet Applications Development.

In addition to school, I worked as a freelance web designer for a local business during the summer of 2017 and have been employed at a start-up web design and software company since May 2017. In this position, I have planned and built websites for the company's clients, helped create the company's website, managed online and offline marketing efforts, and been involved in establishing company processes and procedures. In other words, I've been able to use my background in journalism (writing and content creation) with marketing (social media and e-marketing campaigns) with my web design and development skills. It's been the perfect opportunity.

Web design and development can be as frustrating as it is fun, but that's why I love it. You're never done learning, there'll always be a new technological challenge to overcome or a new language/language to learn. I like knowing that, whatever I do in the design and development world, I won't be bored. I don't have a clear path for my future. I've ultimately decided that long-term plans concerning my career are futile as I'm only going to throw it all out later. I do know I want to design websites (or even applications) that are accessible and provide positive user experiences, especially when the website is reliant on a database for user interactions. Currently, my goal is to get more experience with front-end and back-end programming languages so that I can work in different areas of web and application development.
